## If video is how we prove something happened, what happens when any video can be made to happen?

note:
**Talk:** Read the question aloud. Pause. Let it land before moving on.

This framing question structures the entire lecture. The first half examines what video's epistemic status actually rests on (and why the "apocalypse" narrative gets it wrong). The second half pivots to what synthetic video enables beyond deception. Students should leave not with an answer but with a more sophisticated version of the question — and with ideas for testing these systems themselves.

# 1. Video as witness

note:
**Talk:** Let's start with what makes video different from every other medium we've looked at so far.

This section establishes the stakes. Before we can talk about what threatens video's credibility, students need to feel why it matters. Keep this section relatively brief — it's a setup, not the main argument.

---

<!-- slide: visual | brief: V2 -->

![[W7_7AAVDH27_V2.png]]

https://youtu.be/-_a-NSS5eyU?si=7peZ0pRKM0aS_rCa

note:
**Talk:** Point to the image. "This is a still from [specific footage]. This moment mattered because it was on video. Not because someone described it. Not because someone photographed it. Because we could watch it unfold." Ask: what does video give us that a photograph doesn't?

Video carries more epistemic weight than a still image because it unfolds in time. It captures gesture, voice, duration, context. A photograph freezes a moment; a video seems to let us *witness*. This is why video evidence carries particular weight in courts, in journalism, in political accountability. The history here is long — think of the Zapruder film, the Rodney King footage, smartphone recordings of police violence. In each case, video was treated not as a representation but as a *trace* of what happened. That assumption — that video is indexical, that it records rather than constructs — is what's at stake when we talk about synthetic video.

Then transition: "This is exactly the question that Joshua Habgood-Coote addresses."

# 2. Deflating the apocalypse

note:
**Talk:** The epistemic apocalypse thesis says deepfakes will destroy our ability to trust any recording. Habgood-Coote says that's wrong — and his reasons are instructive.

This is the heaviest intellectual section of the lecture. Take your time. The key move is the deflation — students expect techno-panic, and instead they get a more interesting argument about social epistemology.

---

<!-- slide: visual | brief: V3 -->
![TODO: V3 — Two-panel SVG: Apocalypse Thesis (cascade) vs Habgood-Coote's Deflation (network)](placeholder)

note:
**Talk:** Walk through left panel first — this is the narrative you've probably heard. "Deepfakes exist, therefore we can't trust recordings, therefore epistemic collapse." It's a domino chain. Now look at the right. Habgood-Coote's point: trust in recordings was never held up by the technology alone. It rests on social norms, institutional verification, context of circulation, and a long history of dealing with manipulation. Deepfakes stress this network, but they don't collapse it.

Habgood-Coote's central argument is that the epistemic apocalypse thesis rests on a misunderstanding — it assumes that our trust in video recordings is grounded in the technical difficulty of faking them. But that was never true. Photographs have been manipulated since the 19th century; film has been edited, staged, and fabricated since its invention. What makes recordings trustworthy is not their technical properties but the social and institutional practices that surround them: chains of custody, journalistic verification, legal standards of evidence, contextual plausibility. This is a social epistemology argument, not a media theory argument, and it's important that students grasp the distinction. The diagram helps because it makes visible the structural difference between a linear collapse narrative and a distributed network of trust.

## The liar's dividend

The mere existence of deepfakes benefits liars — authentic evidence can now be dismissed as fabrication.

note:
**Talk:** "This is the mechanism of real harm. Not that fakes fool us — but that fakes give liars an alibi." Emphasise "dismissed" — the harm is in the denial, not the deception.

The liar's dividend (Chesney & Citron, 2019) is the concept students should walk away with from this section. It reframes the deepfakes problem: the primary harm is not that people are fooled by fabricated video, but that the *possibility* of fabrication gives bad actors a ready-made defence against authentic evidence. "That video is a deepfake" becomes a universal get-out clause. This is a double-bind: synthetic video harms both by deceiving and by licensing denial. Connect this to your book's argument about machine-mediated vision — the history of photographic truth was always contested, but the new development is that the contestation itself has become weaponised. Note for students: this concept will be relevant to their e-portfolio reflections.

# 3. Synthetic selves

note:
**Talk:** Pivot. "So the apocalypse narrative is overblown. But synthetic video is very real and very widespread. What is it actually *doing* in culture?"

This is the turn in the lecture — from what synthetic video threatens to what it enables. The energy should shift from critical analysis to curiosity about practice.

---

note:
**Talk:** Walk through the examples. "A VTuber — nobody thinks this is a real person. A virtual influencer with millions of followers — the audience knows. A face-swap used as artistic practice. An AI avatar presenting a corporate message." Ask: "In which of these is anyone being *deceived*?"

This slide reframes synthetic video as a creative and cultural medium rather than a weapon. The examples are chosen for range: entertainment (VTubers), commercial culture (virtual influencers like Lil Miquela), artistic practice (face-swap art), and functional application (AI avatars for communication). In none of these cases is deception the point — which means the epistemic framework from Section 2, while important, doesn't capture everything that's happening. Students from media, arts, and marketing backgrounds will connect to different examples here. The question "in which of these is anyone being deceived?" is genuinely open — it invites students to notice that deception is a spectrum, and that much of synthetic video culture operates in a space where authenticity is beside the point.

## "Avataring" — not a thing you have, but a process you do

note:
**Talk:** "Tom Willma proposes we stop thinking of the avatar as a noun — a digital puppet — and start thinking of avataring as a verb. It's something you *do*, a mode of being present digitally that isn't about projecting your identity but about experimenting with embodiment."

Willma (2025) proposes de-nominalising "avatar" into a verb — shifting from the avatar as an object (a digital representation of a fixed identity) to avataring as a process (a mode of performed digital presence that doesn't necessarily project or represent the "real" self). This is a useful reframing for students because it moves the conversation beyond "is this authentic?" to "what kind of presence does this enable?" It connects to the practical session: when students create an AI avatar of themselves, they're not making a copy — they're doing something new, and the interesting questions are about what that something is. This reading is optional/supplementary, so don't over-invest, but the single concept is worth anchoring on screen.

## For the workshops

note:
**Talk:** "Three workflows, three ways to generate video. Workflow 1: you give it a still image and it generates motion — image to video. Workflow 2: you have a reference video and transfer its motion onto a different subject. Workflow 3: you type text and an avatar speaks it. All three run in ComfyUI, which you've used before for image generation."

This is the most practical slide in the deck and should be clear enough that a student watching the recording can orient themselves for the workshop. Each of the three panels should show the workflow name, a brief description of what goes in and what comes out, and ideally an example. Keep the explanation short — the point is orientation, not instruction. The workshop itself will provide the hands-on guidance. Mention that all three workflows are available as ComfyUI node graphs that they'll load and modify. If relevant, note any setup they need to do beforehand (downloading models, etc.).

## Yes, I can generate X — but what if I tried Y?

note:
**Talk:** "This is your challenge for the workshop. Don't just follow the workflow — come with a question. What happens if you feed the output of one workflow into another? What happens if you give it something it wasn't designed for? What breaks, and what does the breaking reveal?" Pause. "See you in the seminar."

This closing frame is deliberately open. The mini-project (coming in a few weeks) will ask students to chain these capabilities together with intentionality. For now, the goal is to plant the seed of critical-creative experimentation — using these tools not to produce expected outputs but to probe their boundaries. The best student work in modules like this tends to come from questions, not from proficiency. End on this note. Don't summarise the lecture — the question is the takeaway.
